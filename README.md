## Overview

Batutut, also known by the Vietnamese term Người Rừng ('forest people'), is a proposed hominid cryptid of the rainforests of Vietnam, Laos and parts of Cambodia. Witnesses describe a roughly six-foot-tall, bipedal, hair-covered being similar in form to descriptions of Sasquatch, Yeti and Orang Pendek. Reports increased during and after the Vietnam War, when American soldiers reported aggressive hairy bipeds (often called 'rock apes') in the Annamite Range. Vietnamese biologist Tran Hong Viet of the Pedagogic University of Hanoi reported finding and casting large humanoid footprints in Sa Thay District (Kon Tum Province) in 1982 during a post-war biodiversity inventory. The Vu Quang Nature Reserve, where the saola (Pseudoryx nghetinhensis) was scientifically described in 1992, is often cited as a candidate habitat. Mainstream zoologists treat Batutut reports as likely a mix of folklore, misidentified known primates (gibbons, macaques), and wartime stress, while a small number of cryptozoologists hold open the possibility of an unknown great ape.

## Theories & Explanations

### Scientific

**Unknown great-ape hypothesis** _(Cryptozoologists including Loren Coleman, Some researchers in the Heuvelmans tradition)_

Người Rừng / Batutut could represent an undescribed Southeast Asian primate, given the region's continued discovery of large mammals (e.g. saola in 1992, large-antlered muntjac in 1994) and the dense, poorly surveyed Annamite forests.

### Skeptical

**Misidentification of known primates** _(Mainstream primatologists working in the region)_

Most Người Rừng reports are likely misidentifications of known primates (gibbons, macaques, douc langurs) and bears, often under stressful conditions (war, hunting).

**Cultural and wartime construct** _(Folklorists of military legend)_

The cryptid reflects long-standing Southeast Asian wild-man folklore reinforced by Vietnam War-era stress and folklore transmission among soldiers.
